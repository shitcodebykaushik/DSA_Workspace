Page 1: Physical Features & Nomenclature

Physical Features & Ancient Composition of Punjab

Etymology and Historical Nomenclature

The geographical boundaries of Punjab have consistently evolved across distinct historical eras, drastically altering its name within classical texts:

  • Etymology: Derived from two Persian words—Panj (Five) and Ab (Water)—translating directly to the "Land of Five Rivers" (Sutlej, Beas, Ravi, Chenab, Jhelum).
  • Rigvedic Era: Referred to as Sapta Sindhu (Land of Seven Rivers), which included the primary five along with the Sindhu (Indus) and the now-extinct Saraswati.
  • Puranas & Epics (Ramayana/Mahabharata): Formally documented as Panchnad.
  • Greek Accounts: Named Pentapotamia by Hellenistic chroniclers following Alexander’s campaigns.
  • Hiuen Tsang (Chinese Traveler): Documented the region as Taki after a dominant ruling tribe.
  • Mughal & Sikh Rule: Identified as the Province of Lahore under Akbar and later the Lahore Kingdom during the reign of Maharaja Ranjit Singh.

The Strategic Value of the Doabs

The physical terrain of Punjab is split into fertile interfluves (plains between two rivers) known as Doabs. Akbar established this nomenclature by combining the first syllables of the bordering rivers:

Topographical Classification

  • The Shivalik Hills: The narrow, sub-mountainous northern zone spanning Gurdaspur, Hoshiarpur, Rupnagar, and Nawanshahr.
  • The Plains (Alluvial): Divided into Majha (the central Bari Doab; cultural heartland), Doaba (the Bist-Jalandhar area, highly prosperous), and Malwa (the expansive plains south of the Sutlej River including Ludhiana, Patiala, Bhatinda, Ferozepur, Mansa, and Sangrur).
  • The Semi-Arid Southwestern Zone: Bordering Rajasthan (Fazilka, Muktsar).

Page 2: Archaeology & Early Invasions

Archaeology, The Vedic Period & Early Invasions

Indus Valley Civilization (IVC) Sites in Punjab

Punjab’s fertile alluvial soil, continuously replenished by Himalayan rivers, sustained some of the earliest urban footprints of South Asia. Key archaeological sites found in the state include:

  • Ropar (Rupnagar): Located on the banks of the Sutlej River. Excavated post-independence by Y.D. Sharma (1953). It provides a continuous cultural sequence from Harappan to Medieval eras. Notable findings include copper axes, distinct steatite seals, and an unusual burial site where a human was interred alongside a dog.
  • Sunet: Situated in Ludhiana; known for significant yields of Yaudheya coins and ancient clay sealings.
  • Sanghol (Ucha Pind): Located in Fatehgarh Sahib; famous for Kushan-era Buddhist stupas, pillars, and exquisite stone sculptures.
  • Kotla Nihang Khan: An early-phase Harappan settlement located in Rupnagar.

The Vedic Crucible

As Indo-Aryan tribes migrated toward the subcontinent, they chose ancient Punjab as their foundational settlement. The vast majority of the Rig Veda hymns were composed on the riverbanks of the Sapta Sindhu. The region quickly evolved into a prime center for Vedic philosophy, jurisprudence, and language. Taxila (historically part of greater Punjab) became an intellectual hub where the grammarian Panini compiled the Ashtadhyayi.

Classical Invasions & The Battle of Hydaspes (326 BCE)

Because of its unique location, the northwestern passes served as the main entry point for foreign military campaigns into mainland India.

Northwestern Mountain Passes (Khyber/Bolan) ──> Gateway to Punjab Plain ──> Main Entry for Invaders
  • Darius I (Persian): Annexed parts of the Indus valley in the late 6th Century BCE.
  • Alexander the Great (Macedonian): Marched across the Hindu Kush in 326 BCE.
  • The Battle of Hydaspes: Fought on the banks of the Jhelum River (Hydaspes in Greek). King Porus (Paurava) fiercely resisted Alexander's forces. Impressed by Porus's bravery, Alexander restored his kingdom and extended his territory.
  • The Retreat: Alexander's troops mutinied at the Beas River (Hyphasis), refusing to march further east to face the Nanda Empire, which forced him to turn backward.

Page 3: Medieval Punjab

Medieval Punjab under the Sultanate & Mughals

The Ghaznavid and Ghorid Conquests

During the medieval transition, Punjab shifted from a defensive frontier under the Hindu Shahi Dynasty (represented by Kings Jaipal and Anandpal) to a key province within successive Islamic empires.

  • Mahmud of Ghazni: Defeated Jaipal at the Battle of Peshawar (1001 CE). This shattered the Hindu Shahi defense system and established Ghaznavid administrative bases inside Lahore.
  • Battles of Tarain (1191 & 1192 CE): Fought between Mu'izz ad-Din Muhammad Ghori and the Rajput confederacy led by Prithviraj Chauhan. The second battle firmly secured the gateway of Punjab and Delhi for the Ghurid Empire.

The Delhi Sultanate Era

Throughout the Mamluk, Khilji, Tughlaq, Sayyid, and Lodi Dynasties, Punjab remained strategically vital. It stood as the first line of defense against Mongol incursions and served as the primary trade highway connecting Delhi with Central Asia.

  • The Mongol Threat: Balban strengthened northwestern forts like Bhatinda, Sunam, and Samana to counter Mongol raids.
  • Economic Footprint: Abohar grew into a key trade station along the major Multan-Delhi commercial corridor.

The Mughal-Afghan Transition

  • First Battle of Panipat (1526 CE): Zahir-ud-din Muhammad Babur defeated Ibrahim Lodi, ending the Delhi Sultanate and founding the Mughal Empire. Babur initiated this campaign by securing his hold over central Punjab.
  • Administrative Setup: Under Emperor Akbar's Ain-i-Akbari, Punjab was organized into the Subah of Lahore and the Subah of Multan. The administrative subdivisions were split along the natural boundaries of the five major Doabs.

Page 4: Sikh Gurus (Early Foundation)

The Sikh Gurus — Foundation to Institutionalization

Guru Nanak Dev (Langar/Sangat) ──> Guru Angad (Gurmukhi) ──> Guru Amar Das (Manji System) ──> Guru Ram Das (Amritsar)

1. Guru Nanak Dev Ji (1469–1539)

  • Birth: Talwandi Rai Bhoe (now Nankana Sahib, Pakistan) in the Kartika Purnima week.
  • Parents: Mehta Kalu (father) and Mata Tripta Ji (mother).
  • Key Philosophies: Kirat Karo (honest labor), Naam Japna (remembering God), Vand Chhako (sharing earnings). He introduced Langar (the community kitchen) and Sangat (the holy congregation) to dismantle caste hierarchies.
  • Divine Travels (Udasis): Completed four extensive journeys across the subcontinent and the Middle East to spread monotheism (Ik Onkar).
  • City Founded: Kartarpur (on the banks of the Ravi River) where he spent his final years.

2. Guru Angad Dev Ji (1504–1552)

  • Original Name: Bhai Lehna Ji.
  • Script Standardization: Formalized and standardized the Gurmukhi Script, ensuring spiritual and secular literature was accessible to the public.
  • Physical Education: Established Mall Akhara systems to promote physical fitness alongside spiritual discipline.
  • City Founded: Khadur Sahib.

3. Guru Amar Das Ji (1479–1574)

  • Administrative System: Devised the Manji System (22 distinct administrative zones) and the Piri System to appoint representatives to spread the core teachings of Sikhism.
  • Social Reforms: Strongly campaigned against the practices of Sati and Purdah, while actively encouraging widow remarriage.
  • Architectural Legacy: Constructed the Baoli (stepwell featuring 84 steps) at Goindwal Sahib.
  • Anand Sahib: Composed the celebrated hymn Anand Sahib, which is recited during major ceremonies.

4. Guru Ram Das Ji (1534–1581)

  • Original Name: Bhai Jetha Ji.
  • City Founded: Ramdaspur, which later became known as Amritsar. He excavated the sacred tanks of Santokhsar and Amritsar.
  • Lavan: Composed the four-stanza wedding hymn (Lavan) to institutionalize the Sikh marriage ceremony, Anand Karaj.
  • Masand System: Created a network of representatives to collect voluntary contributions (Dasvandh) to fund community infrastructure projects.

Page 5: Sikh Gurus (Consolidation)

The Sikh Gurus — Consolidation to Martyrdom

5. Guru Arjan Dev Ji (1563–1606)

  • Compilation of Adi Granth (1604): Compiled the primary Sikh scripture, the Adi Granth, preserving the compositions of the first five Gurus alongside verses from Bhakti and Sufi saints like Kabir, Baba Farid, and Ravidas. Bhai Gurdas Ji served as the primary scribe.
  • Harmandir Sahib: Designed and oversaw the construction of Harmandir Sahib (The Golden Temple) in Amritsar. He invited the Sufi saint Mian Mir to lay its foundation stone to emphasize interfaith harmony.
  • Cities Founded: Tarn Taran Sahib, Kartarpur (Doaba), and Sri Hargobindpur.
  • Martyrdom: Arrested under the orders of Mughal Emperor Jahangir for refusing to alter the Adi Granth text. He became the First Martyr of the Sikh Faith in 1606.

6. Guru Hargobind Ji (1595–1644)

  • Miri and Piri Concept: Introduced a dual-sword policy symbolizing Miri (temporal, political authority) and Piri (spiritual authority), adapting the community to defend against state persecution.
  • Akal Takht: Constructed the Akal Takht (Throne of the Timeless One) in 1609 directly facing Harmandir Sahib to handle temporal and political matters.
  • Fortification: Built the fortress of Lohgarh in Amritsar and maintained a standing defensive army.
  • City Founded: Kiratpur Sahib.

7. Guru Har Rai Ji (1630–1661)

  • Alliances: Offered medical aid to Dara Shikoh, the liberal eldest son of Emperor Shah Jahan, during the Mughal war of succession.
  • Environmental/Medical Focus: Established a prominent Ayurvedic herbal medicine hospital and research center at Kiratpur Sahib.

8. Guru Har Krishan Ji (1556–1664)

  • Youngest Guru: Assumed office at the age of five; affectionately remembered as "Bala Pritam".
  • Humanitarian Work: Dedicated his time to treating cholera and smallpox epidemics in Delhi, where he eventually contracted smallpox and passed away.
  • Historical Site: The place where he stayed in Delhi is preserved as the Gurdwara Bangla Sahib.

Page 6: Khalsa & Banda Singh Bahadur

The Birth of Khalsa and the Era of Banda Singh Bahadur

9. Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji (1621–1675)

  • Titles: Honored as Hind Di Chadar (Shield of India) for defending religious freedom.
  • Sacrifice: Executed at Chandni Chowk, Delhi, under the orders of Aurangzeb for defending the religious rights of Kashmiri Pandits who sought his protection.
  • Historical Gurdwaras: Gurdwara Sis Ganj Sahib marks the site of his execution, while Gurdwara Rakab Ganj Sahib marks his cremation site in Delhi.
  • City Founded: Anandpur Sahib (originally named Chakk Nanaki).

10. Guru Gobind Singh Ji (1666–1708)

  • Birth: Patna Sahib, Bihar (1666).
  • Creation of the Khalsa (March 30, 1699): Founded the Khalsa Panth at Anandpur Sahib during Baisakhi. He initiated the Panj Pyares (Five Beloved Ones) and introduced the mandatory 5 Ks:

  • Literary Contributions: Authored the Zafarnama (Epistle of Victory) sent directly to Aurangzeb from Dina Kanjpura. His major writings are compiled in the Dasam Granth.
  • Major Battles: Fought battles against hill chiefs and Mughal forces, including the Battles of Bhangani, Nadaun, Anandpur Sahib, Chamkaur Sahib, and Muktsar (Khidrane Di Dhab).
  • Zonal Succession: Declared the line of living Gurus complete, passing eternal spiritual authority to the Guru Granth Sahib Ji. He passed away at Nanded (Hazur Sahib) in 1708.

The Military Campaigns of Banda Singh Bahadur (1708–1716)

  • Rise: Commissioned by Guru Gobind Singh Ji at Nanded to lead the Sikh military forces against oppression in Punjab.
  • Battle of Chappar Chiri (1710): Defeated the Mughal Governor of Sirhind, Wazir Khan, to avenge the executions of the younger sons of Guru Gobind Singh Ji (Chhoti Sahibzade).
  • Sovereignty: Established his capital at Mukhlispur (renamed Lohgarh) and issued coins in the names of Guru Nanak Dev Ji and Guru Gobind Singh Ji.
  • Execution: Captured during the siege of Gurdas Nangal and executed in Delhi under Mughal Emperor Farrukhsiyar in 1716.

Page 7: Misl Period & Holocausts

The Misl Period & Twin Holocausts (18th Century)

Following the execution of Banda Singh Bahadur, Punjab entered a period of political fragmentation, facing repeated invasions from Persia (Nadir Shah) and Afghanistan (Ahmed Shah Abdali).

The Twin Holocausts (Ghallughara)

  • Chhota Ghallughara (First Holocaust - 1746): A surprise military assault carried out by Lakhpat Rai (minister of Mughal Governor Yahiya Khan) at Kahnuwan (Gurdaspur district), resulting in the deaths of over 7,000 Sikh soldiers and civilians.
  • Vadda Ghallughara (Second Holocaust - 1762): A massive surprise attack launched by Ahmed Shah Abdali near Kup Rohira (Malerkotla district), resulting in the loss of an estimated 20,000 to 35,000 Sikh lives in a single day.

Page 8: The Empire of Ranjit Singh

The Sikh Empire — Reign of Maharaja Ranjit Singh

Consolidated Rise to Power (1799–1839)

  • Birth: November 2, 1780, at Gujranwala, to Mahan Singh of the Sukerchakia Misl.
  • Capture of Lahore (1799): Defeated the Bhangi rulers of Lahore, turning the city into his formal political capital. He was formally crowned Maharaja of Punjab on April 12, 1801.
  • Religious Capital: Captured Amritsar from the Bhangi Misl in 1802, bringing the two major cities of Punjab under unified rule.

Key Treaties and Strategic Borders

  • Treaty of Amritsar (1809): Signed between Maharaja Ranjit Singh and Charles Metcalfe (representing the British East India Company). This treaty established the Sutlej River as the permanent geopolitical boundary between the Sikh Empire and British expansionist territory.
  • Tripartite Treaty (1838): Signed among Maharaja Ranjit Singh, Shah Shuja (the exiled Afghan monarch), and the British administration to stabilize the northwestern frontier.
Treaty of Amritsar (1809) ──> Settled Sutlej River as the Boundary ──> Diverted Expansion Northwest (Multan, Kashmir, Peshawar)

Military Modernization and Expansion

  • Fauj-i-Khas: The specialized elite wing of the Fauj-i-Ain (regular standing army), trained by European mercenary officers like Jean-Baptiste Ventura (Italy) and Jean-Francois Allard (France) to match European infantry standards.
  • Key Generals: Hari Singh Nalwa (the legendary frontier commander who fortified the Khyber Pass), Dewan Mokham Chand, and Misr Diwan Chand.
  • Territorial Conquests: Successfully captured Multan (1818), Kashmir (1819), and Peshawar (1834), expanding the empire's borders right up to the Khyber Pass.

Secular Administration

Ranjit Singh’s government, known as Sarkar-i-Khalsa, operated on secular principles. His trusted Prime Minister was Dhian Singh Dogra, his Foreign Minister was Fakir Azizuddin, and his Finance Minister was Dewan Dinanath. He financed the gilding of Harmandir Sahib with gold plate, giving rise to its popular name, the Golden Temple. The Maharaja passed away in 1839, initiating a period of political instability.

Page 9: Anglo-Sikh Wars & Annexation

Anglo-Sikh Wars & British Annexation

Following the death of Maharaja Ranjit Singh, internal palace intrigues weakened the court, leading to direct military confrontations with the British East India Company.

The First Anglo-Sikh War (1845–1846)

The war was marked by internal betrayal from Prime Minister Lal Singh and Army Commander Tej Singh.

  • Battle of Mudki (1845): British victory under Sir Hugh Gough.
  • Battle of Ferozeshah (1845): A hard-fought engagement resulting in heavy casualties on both sides.
  • Battle of Aliwal (1846): British forces commanded by Sir Harry Smith secured a decisive victory.
  • Battle of Sobraon (1846): The decisive turning point of the war. Sardar Sham Singh Attariwala fought heroically to the end, refusing to surrender despite betrayal from his senior army commanders.
  • Outcome - Treaty of Lahore (1846): The British placed a resident at the Lahore Durbar. Maharaja Duleep Singh (the minor youngest son of Ranjit Singh) was recognized as ruler, and Jammu & Kashmir was carved out and sold to Gulab Singh Dogra under the Treaty of Amritsar (1846).

The Second Anglo-Sikh War (1848–1849)

Sparked by a localized rebellion in Multan led by Governor Dewan Mulraj, the unrest quickly grew into a widespread war for liberation.

  • Battle of Ramnagar (1848): A minor cavalry skirmish fought along the Chenab River.
  • Battle of Chillianwala (1849): A strategic victory for the Sikh forces under Sher Singh Attariwala. This battle resulted in some of the heaviest losses the British army had experienced in the subcontinent, leading to the immediate recall of Commander Hugh Gough.
  • Battle of Gujrat (1849): Known historically as the Battle of Guns because of the heavy artillery duels that decided the outcome. The British won a decisive victory.

The Annexation of Punjab (March 29, 1849)

  • The Proclamation: Lord Dalhousie, the Governor-General of India, officially annexed Punjab into the British Empire.
  • Board of Administration: A three-member administrative board was established to govern the newly formed province, led by Sir Henry Lawrence (President), John Lawrence, and Charles Mansel.
  • Duleep Singh’s Fate: The young Maharaja was pensioned off and sent to England, and the Koh-i-Noor diamond was taken from the treasures of Lahore and presented to Queen Victoria.

Page 10: Freedom Struggle & Modern Era

Freedom Movements & Modern Reorganization (1849–1966)

Socio-Religious and Socio-Political Resistance Movements

  • Namdhari / Kuka Movement (1857): Founded initially by Balak Singh and dynamically expanded by Baba Ram Singh. They championed a strict boycott of British goods, British educational institutions, and the state postal network, making it an early pioneer of non-cooperation. In 1872, 66 Kuka activists were executed by being blown from cannons at Malerkotla.
  • Singh Sabha Movement (1873): Launched in Amritsar to counter the influence of Christian missionary conversions and Westernization. It focused on promoting Punjabi literature and establishing a network of Khalsa schools and colleges.
  • Ghadar Party (1913): Formed in San Francisco, USA, by expatriate Indian revolutionaries, led by Lala Hardayal, Baba Sohan Singh Bhakna (the founding President), and Kartar Singh Sarabha. They published the weekly revolutionary newspaper Ghadar to spark an armed uprising against British rule.
  • Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (April 13, 1919): A peaceful crowd gathered at Jallianwala Bagh on Baisakhi to protest the arrests of Dr. Saifuddin Kitchlew and Dr. Satyapal under the Rowlatt Act. Brigadier-General Reginald Dyer ordered troops to open fire on the trapped crowd. Sardar Udham Singh later assassinated Michael O'Dwyer (the Lieutenant Governor of Punjab during the massacre) at Caxton Hall, London, in 1940.
  • The Akali Movement / Gurdwara Reform Movement (1920–1925): A non-violent campaign launched to reclaim control of historic Gurdwaras from corrupt hereditary priests (Mahants). This led directly to the enactment of the landmark Sikh Gurdwaras Act, 1925, which established the Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee (SGPC).

The Partition and Post-Independence Reorganization

  • The Partition (1947): Sir Cyril Radcliffe drew the border that split Punjab along the line of the Bari Doab. This divided the region into West Punjab (which went to Pakistan) and East Punjab (which remained with India).
  • PEPSU (1948): The Patiala and East Punjab States Union was created by combining eight princely states (Patiala, Nabha, Jind, Faridkot, Kapurthala, Kalsia, Malerkotla, and Nalagarh). Its capital was established at Patiala, and Maharaja Yadavindra Singh served as Rajpramukh. PEPSU was merged into Punjab in 1956.
  • The Punjabi Suba Movement & Reorganization (1966): Led by Master Tara Singh and Sant Fateh Singh, the movement campaigned for a state organized around the Punjabi language. Following the recommendations of the Shah Commission, the modern state of Punjab was created on November 1, 1966. The Hindi-speaking areas were carved out to form Haryana, while the hilly northern districts were transferred to Himachal Pradesh, with Chandigarh established as a shared Union Territory and capital.
